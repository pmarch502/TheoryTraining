Lesson 19: Dominant Seventh

As we’ve been walking through these lessons, we’ve tried to follow a logical progression. If you’ve picked up on that, you might have thought that the next logical chord after the major seventh would be the minor seventh. That’s good thinking, but it would be better for us to make a slight detour to the dominant seventh first.

The dominant seventh brings tension and pull—classic for leading back to I in worship choruses (ii-V7-I).

The actual dominant seventh formula is defined as:

  • Root
  • Major third above root: an interval of 4 half steps, W+W. E.g., C to E.
  • Perfect fifth above root: an interval of 7 half steps, a major third + a minor third, (W+W)+(W+H). E.g., D to A.
  • Minor seventh above root: an interval of 10 half steps, a major third + a minor third + minor third, (W+W)+(W+H)+(W+H). (We might also call it a perfect fifth + a minor third.) E.g., C to B♭.

Again, this definition is very important because it is very precise - it has an important purpose in defining and naming chords. You may have a question, though - if the dominant seventh formula has a minor seventh interval in it, why isn’t it called a minor seventh? That’s a great question!

Remember back when we learned triads, we had major chords and minor chords? We discovered that when we had a major chord, it was just kind of assumed - we didn’t need to say major. But, when we said ‘minor’ we were specifically talking about a ♭3. On a chord chart, a C major most commonly appears as simply ‘C’. But, a C minor gets the decoration ‘Cm’. So, that ‘m’ (lowercase) decoration needs to mean only one thing - a ♭3. If we tried to also use ‘m’ in relation to a seventh chord, we’d never know which was which! So, when you see an ‘m’, it is always talking about the ♭3.

But, we’re talking about seventh chords now. Remember in the last lesson we said a major seventh chord might look like this: CM7. The ‘M’ (uppercase) is specifically referring to the 7 (not the 3). So, major triads don’t use the ‘M’ (normally) because it’s reserved for the seventh - specifically designating the 7 of the chord root’s major scale. When it is the ♭7 (like in the dominant seventh), we use no decoration at all.

This may be a little confusing, but it is actually quite smart - at least as far as creating an unambiguous naming convention for chord qualities. A lower case ‘m’ means a ♭3 and an upper case ‘M’ means a major 7. Our next lesson will, hopefully, help make this a little clearer.

Here is one example of a dominant seventh:

C dominant seventh: C - E - G - B♭

1   2   3   4   5   6   7   8
C   D   E   F   G   A   B   C
^       ^       ^       ♭

When a dominant seventh appears on a chord chart, it only appears with one chord quality decoration: C7 (we have a major triad (no decoration) and it’s not the 7 - it’s the ♭7 (no decoration))

When we talk about a dominant seventh according to scale degrees, we would call it a 1-3-5-♭7 (or, 1-3-5-7♭ - people say it both ways). (Remember - these numbers are scale degrees from the major scale of the root of the chord.)

Definitions

Dominant Seventh
A seventh chord with major third, perfect fifth, and minor seventh (1-3-5-♭7), creating strong resolution tendency to the tonic.
Key
Refers to the tonal center or scale upon which a musical composition is primarily based. It establishes the tonic (the central note) and defines the set of pitches, intervals, and chords that predominate in the piece, providing a sense of hierarchy, resolution, and harmonic framework.
Major Third
Interval of four half steps (two whole steps), producing a bright, open sound (e.g., C to E).
Minor Seventh
Interval of ten half steps (perfect fifth + minor third), offering smooth, melancholic resolution (e.g., D to C).
Perfect Fifth
Interval of seven half steps, providing stability and consonance (e.g., C to G).
Scale
An ordered sequence of notes, typically spanning an octave, arranged in ascending or descending order according to a specific pattern of intervals (whole steps and half steps, or tones and semitones). It serves as the foundational pitch collection for melodies, harmonies, and improvisation within a musical composition or tradition.
Triad
A three-note chord consisting of a root, third, and fifth, forming the basic building block of harmony.
